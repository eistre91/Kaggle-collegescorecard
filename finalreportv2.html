A Data-driven Approach to School Selection

Erik Istre

January 13, 2016

Youre a high school senior scared about what shes going to do for college. Maybe you worked really hard but could never get your mind wrapped around Calculus. Maybe you spent so much time on extracurriculars that you didnt do as well academically as you could have. Maybe you simply fooled around too much. And now youve received some acceptance letters from a few colleges, but none of them are Harvard or University of Chicago. None of them is a big name that gives you a head start on your future. How do you choose among universities without obvious advantages?

Then it sounds like youre a regular high school student.

The college experience is complex. Along with classes, youll grow in numerous person ways. Its impossible to know what college is best for you. However, do not fear your future. Im here to help the choice seem a little less mysterious and impossible.

If going to college is ultimately about getting a better career, thats something we can focus on. To make this more tangible, well consider what features of a school predict better future earnings. This will help you discover what questions to ask about a school and will make you more confident in your final choice.

Well be using data provided by the Department of Education as part of their College Scorecard project.

Youre Not Doomed

Your future is not set in stone just because you didnt get into one of the most highly selective schools. It actually looks pretty open:

Each point in this graph is a school with the y axis giving the median earnings of students who have attended that school, and the x axis is giving what percentage of students a school admits of those who applied. Ive highlighted the block of schools that admit between 30% and 99% of students who apply and have earnings ranging from $15000 to $60000. The vast majority of schools fall into this block and within this block a significant proportion of them occuring between $30000 and $50000.

Another way to say it, the selectivity of a school does not necessarily predict future career incomes. If we calculate the degree of the correlation in this block, we find its almost non-existent. We do see a more significant trend after selectivity crosses the 30% threshold and continues to decrease, but even that is a weak connection.

Are you worried that youll have to graduate with a crushing mountain of debt to eventually end up with a good career? If youre not looking to become something like a doctor or a lawyer (paths known to generate lots of debt and attract some of the brightest) it turns out that there isnt a relationship between the debt you take on and your future earnings. Where

Lets say that youre planning to move away from home for college. You may already have your mind set on a particular city or state that you want to live in for a few years. Perhaps you have no clue except that you want to get away.

What does the data say about what state you should go to school in? This turns out to be a very complicated question to ask of the dataset.

First, the dataset only gives median earnings, but it gives no information about where the students have found employment after graduating. Did they move to state different from where they got their degree?

The geographic mobility of people with college degrees is very high. This is further discussed here Pew Social Trends. Since I have no method to control for this, this lessens the relability of the data presented in this section.

Then we have to consider the cost of living in states. Can we compare incomes between two states given that cost of living varies?

We can control for cost of living but given the other complicating factors, this doesnt tell the whole story. I use data provided by the Tax Foundation to adjust earnings properly. (Tax Foundation Report) The Tax Foundation uses the average national buying power of $100, and then adjusts from there to decide if a place is more or less expensive to live than the national average.

Further, predominant industries are different between states and influence the earning potential of students staying in those states.

If theres a particular company or industry youre interested in, then it makes sense to choose a state or region where that company operates or that industry is available to work in. If thats the case, this section is of limited use to you.

Thus, the following graphs, the first giving the unadjusted 10 year median earnings of students by state, and the second the adjusted earnings taking cost of living into account, should be understood only as another piece of the puzzle and not an answer by themselves.

Some Important Information on Schools

Ill close this article by discussing two things that stand out in the dataset as being well correlated with future earnings. These are whether students tend to remain enrolled at the university they started at after 2 years, and whether the faculty is well-paid. It turns out that these variables are also reasonably correlated with each other, so there is some degree of relationship.

Of course, it is impossible to accurately assess causality here. Do good schools attract faculty members that deserve higher pay, or does a school give their faculty high salaries which causes faculty to be more attached to their jobs?

Here is what we get looking at retention rate at two years:

This variable has a really strong effect on producing higher incomes. I think this may be demonstrating that schools that do well to meet their students needs do well to produce better outcomes for their students.

Note that retention rate at 2 years is non-trivial as very few schools in this dataset manage to maintain 75% or more of their students after two years.

Concluding Remarks

Weve seen that youre probably not doomed when it comes to choosing a school. This alone should ease your fears. And now you also have a few guiding bits of information to help you choose. I know this is a mere drop in the bucket of helping you make the decision to come, but I hope it can seem a little bit less mysterious and little more educated about whats important.
